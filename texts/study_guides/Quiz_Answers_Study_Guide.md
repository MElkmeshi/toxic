## 1. Definitions

| Term | Definition (from content) |
|------|---------------------------|
| **Toxicology** | The study of poisons and their action on the living body; knowledge of poisons, their chemical properties, identification, biological effects and treatment of disease conditions caused by poisons. |
| **Poison / Toxicant** | Any solid, liquid, or gas that, when introduced into or applied to the body, can interfere with the life processes of cells of the organism by its inherent qualities without acting mechanically and irrespective of temperature. |
| **Toxic** | The effects of a poison on a living organism. |
| **Toxin / Biotoxin** | A poison that originates from biological processes — **Zootoxins** (animal), **Bacterial toxins** (endo- or exotoxins), **Phytotoxins** (plant), **Mycotoxins** (fungal). |
| **Toxicosis (Poisoning / Intoxication)** | The disease state that results from exposure to a poison. |
| **Dosage** | The amount of toxicant per unit of animal weight. |
| **Dose** | The total amount of toxicant received per animal. |
| **Toxicity** | The amount of a poison that, under a specific set of conditions, will cause toxic effects. |
| **Acute toxicity** | Effects of a single dose or multiple doses during 24 h (may appear over days/weeks). |
| **Chronic toxicity** | Effects produced by prolonged exposure ≥ 3 months. |
| **Allergy** | Immunological response due to sensitization from a previous dose. |
| **Carcinogenicity** | The agent is responsible for the formation of neoplasia. |
| **Teratogenicity** | Material ingested by the mother at some definite stage in pregnancy produces abnormalities in the offspring. |
| **Highest Non-Toxic Dose (HNTD)** | The largest dose that does not result in haematological/chemical/clinical/pathologic drug-induced alterations. |
| **Toxic Dose-Low (TDL)** | Lowest dose to produce drug-induced alterations; administering twice this dose will **not** result in lethality. |
| **Toxic Dose-High (TDH)** | The dose which will produce drug-induced alterations; administering twice this dose **will** result in lethality. |
| **Lethal Dose (LD)** | The lowest dose that causes drug-induced death in any animal during the period of observation. |
| **Median Lethal Dose (MLD)** | Equivalent to LD₅₀ (used interchangeably). |
| **Effective Dose 50 (ED₅₀)** | Dosage of a drug/therapeutic agent that produces the desired effect in half of a population. |
| **Therapeutic Index (TI)** | TI = LD₅₀ / ED₅₀. |
| **Standard Safety Margin (SSM)** | SSM = LD₁ / ED₉₉. |
| **Maximum Tolerated Dose (MTD)** | Largest dose tolerated; also used as "minimal toxic dose" depending on author. |
| **Threshold Limit Value (TLV)** | The level of toxic agent which does not cause adverse effect on animal or human during working (pasturing) time of environmental dilution. |
| **Hazard / Risk** | Likelihood that a chemical or drug will cause harm under certain conditions. |
| **Bioactivation** | First stage of xenobiotic metabolism — introduction of a recipient (functional) group (OH, COOH, NH₂) into the molecule by oxidation/reduction/hydrolysis. |
| **Lethal synthesis** | The body metabolises a non-toxic xenobiotic into a more poisonous metabolite. **Examples:** parathion → paraoxon; fluoroacetate → fluorocitrate. |
| **Curie (Ci)** | Unit of radioactivity = rate of disintegration of 3.7 × 10¹⁰/sec (= disintegration rate of 1 g of radium). |
| **Specific activity** | Activity per unit mass (Ci·g⁻¹). |
| **Rad** | Unit of absorbed dose = 100 ergs/g = 10⁻² J·kg⁻¹. Applies to all forms of ionizing radiation. |
| **Roentgen** | Unit of radiation exposure — amount of radiation producing 1 esu of charge per cm³ of air at STP (X- or γ-rays only). 1 R = 2.58 × 10⁻⁴ C·kg⁻¹. |
| **Coulomb (C)** | Unit of measuring amount of electric current/charge. |
| **MeV (Million electron Volt)** | 1.6 × 10⁻⁶ erg. |
| **Isotopes** | Nuclides with the same atomic number but different mass number. |
| **Radioisotopes** | Atom with unstable combination of protons & neutrons that decays and emits radiation. |
| **Insensible perspiration** | Loss of moisture from a non-sweating body. In temperate climate, body loses ~24 % of metabolic heat through insensible perspiration. |
| **Cellular death / Molecular life** | Cessation of respiration and metabolism of body tissues, soon followed by autolysis and decay. |
| **Somatic death** | The person no longer functions as a unit of society — irreversibly unconscious, cannot communicate. |
| **Brain-stem death** | When vital centres in the medulla are destroyed (trauma, cerebral oedema, haemorrhage, hypoxia, infection) — respiratory motor system fails. **Legally = death.** |
| **Lightning syndrome** | Atmospheric gases heated to 20 000 °C; 1/3 of lightning deaths occur immediately from paralysis of vital centres; survivors develop loss of consciousness, temporary impairment of peripheral/central NS function, skin burns, fractures, gross laceration, fusion of metallic objects in clothes. |
| **Hide-and-Die syndrome** | In old people, ↓ °C may be linked with a psychiatric condition — they hide in corners/cupboards/under furniture, often remove some/all clothing → become hypothermic. |
| **Heat Hematoma** | Severe heat → skull → blood boils out of venous sinuses → external haematoma between burnt bone & dura → brown spongy blood — can mislead as ante-mortem traumatic lesion. |
| **Heat cramps / Miner's cramp / Stoker's cramp** | Painful muscle spasms during/after physical activity in a hot environment → rapid dehydration + loss of salts → painful spasm of voluntary muscles (extremities, abdominal wall). IV physiological saline gives dramatic relief; salt tablets + water are prophylactic. |
| **Sun stroke (Heat stroke)** | No sweating, ↑ body temp in presence of environmental heat stress and absence of other cause of pyrexia; cyanosed flashed face, ↑ depth of respiration, rapid pulse, severe asthenia, convulsion, ↓ BP, hyperthermia, ↑ BUN. |
| **Wallace's rule / Rule of nine** | Extent of burned area: Head & neck 9 %, each fore limb 9 %, trunk (each side) 18 %, hind limbs 18 %, external genital system 1 %. Area is more important than depth; > 50 % burned even 1st degree → poor prognosis. |
| **Cold edema** | Cold diuresis + leaking of plasma into extracellular spaces → haemoconcentration. |
| **Wischnevsky ulcer** | Gastric ulcer that develops in hypothermia (also found in ileum & colon). |
| **Adipocere** | Body fat → hydrolysis → waxy compound similar to soap. Develops in wet conditions over weeks-months. |
| **Mummification** | Bodies in warm dry conditions → tissue dries, leathery & brown; skin stretched over face. Common in desert/newborns/cool dry stores. |
| **Cadaveric spasm** | Rare instantaneous rigor due to active muscular contraction during life in persons under high physical/emotional stress immediately before death. |
| **Rigor mortis** | Irreversible combination of actin + myosin within skeletal/cardiac/involuntary muscle after death (ATP↓, lactic acid↑). |
| **Heat stiffness (heat rigor)** | °C ↑ → coagulation of muscle proteins → rigor mortis comes on rapidly and passes off rapidly. |
| **Cold stiffness** | °C ↓ → rigidity from freezing/icing of synovial fluids → short-time rigor → secondary flaccidity. |
| **Hypostasis / Post-mortem lividity / Staining / Suggillation** | After circulation ceases — plasma → oedema of lower areas + RBCs → pink/bluish zone. Colour varies with cause: CO → cherry pink, cyanide → brick-red, met/sulph-Hb → brown, hypothermia → bright pink. Used to estimate cause of death, time of death, and cadaver position. |
| **Asphyxia** | Literally "absence of pulse"; usually denotes anoxia/hypoxia; in forensic context = obstructive (barrier prevents air access to lungs). |
| **Suboxia / Anoxia** | ↓ O₂ in blood & tissue → ↑ reduced Hb & ↑ CO₂ → stimulation of respiratory centre. |
| **Suffocation** | Absence or reduction of O₂ in inspired air OR mechanical obstruction to air entry at external respiratory orifices (smothering). |
| **Smothering** | Hand or pillow pressed over nose & mouth. |
| **Choking** | Internal obstruction of upper air passages. |
| **Throttling (Manual strangulation)** | Strangulation with one or both hands; external signs are abrasions & disc-shaped bruises on front/sides of neck; classic mode of male-on-female homicide. |
| **Ligature strangulation** | Constricting band tightened around neck — leaves a vital, patterned mark; thin/wire cord → clear-cut, sharp; soft fabric → diffuse, faint. |
| **Hanging** | Form of ligature strangulation where the pressure is produced by the weight of the body itself. |
| **Washerwoman's fingers** | Wrinkled, macerated, blanched skin on hands/feet after few hours in cold water (less in warm) — a non-specific sign of immersion (not proof of drowning). |
| **Diatom test** | Microscopic algae (diatoms) sought in kidney/brain/bone marrow as suggestive (not legally sufficient) evidence of drowning. |
| **Bruise / Contusion** | Damage from blunt injury to small veins/arterioles beneath the surface; almost impossible to inflict on a dead body. |
| **Abrasion** | Most superficial injury — damages only cuticle; forensically most important because it retains the pattern of the causative object. |
| **Laceration** | Splitting/tearing wound from blunt injury — passes through full thickness of skin and bleeds profusely; tissues crushed sandwich-fashion. |
| **Incised wound** | Sharply-cut injury — slashed (length > depth) or stab (depth > length). |
| **Tram-line / Railway-line bruise** | Two parallel lines of bruising with pale undamaged area between → impact of a rod-like weapon. |
| **Café coronary** | Sudden silent death from a food bolus impacted in the larynx → vagal inhibition → cardiac arrest. |
| **Dry starvation** | Deprivation of food **and** water — death from cardiac failure in ~10 days. |
| **Wet starvation** | Deprivation of food only — oedema/ascites, hypoproteinaemia (muscle protein used as energy); complete deprivation kills in 50-60 days; less time in hot climate. |

## 2. Toxicokinetics & Mechanisms

### Routes of absorption (Q on biological membranes)
- A toxicant can pass through biological membranes via **both passive and active transport**.
- Weak **acid** toxicants are absorbed **poorly** from the stomach.
- Weak **alkaline** toxicants are absorbed **highly** from the small intestine.
- Chlorinated hydrocarbons are stored in **fat, with** prolonged toxic effect.

### Phase I & II metabolism
- Phase-I includes **oxidation & reduction** (with hydrolysis as a third).
- Cytochrome-P450 mono-oxygenases are located in the **endoplasmic reticulum**.
- Toxicants containing Nitro/Azo groups are detoxicated by **reduction** reactions.
- Phase-II reactions are mediated by **transferase** enzymes (conjugation with glycine, glucuronic acid, amino acids, acetate, sulphates, glutathione).
- Renal elimination may involve **passive glomerular filtration, active tubular secretion and passive tubular diffusion (all of the above)**.
- Toxin in faeces may be from incomplete GI absorption, salivary excretion, or biliary excretion (**all of the above**).

### Mechanism of action of key toxicants
- **HCN / Cyanide:** binds Fe³⁺ of cytochrome oxidase (Complex IV) → blocks **oxygen transfer** → histotoxic hypoxia. Cyanide also combines with metHb / oxyHb → cyanmetHb.
- **Trivalent arsenicals:** As + SH group of dihydrolipoic acid → inactivates lipoic acid → blocks formation of acetyl-, succinyl- and propionyl-CoA → inhibition of cellular respiration; also capillary dilatation & degeneration.
- **Pentavalent inorganic arsenic:** substitutes for phosphate in oxidative phosphorylation → cellular energy deficit.
- **Pentavalent organic arsenic:** demyelination + axonal degeneration (mechanism unknown).
- **Copper (chronic):** excess Cu stored in lysosomes/mitochondria → oxidises RBC membrane → ↑ fragility → haemolysis & hepatocellular necrosis; also oxidises Hb → metHb.
- **Fluoride:** high affinity for Ca → replaces hydroxyl groups in hydroxyapatite → skeletal fluorosis (exostosis or osteoporosis); on developing teeth → damages ameloblasts/odontoblasts → enamel defects.
- **Molybdenum:** promotes hepatic Cu excretion (forms readily-excreted Cu-Mo complex) → clinical Cu deficiency.
- **Mercury (inorganic):** binds SH groups → enzyme inhibition + protein precipitation; direct tissue necrosis & renal tubular necrosis.
- **Mercury (organic alkyl):** lipophilic → CNS accumulation → degeneration & necrosis.
- **Ethylene glycol (anti-freeze):** oxidised to **oxalic acid** (acidotic; nephrotoxic).
- **Carbon monoxide (CO):** competes with O₂ for Hb; affinity 240× greater than O₂; forms COHb → prevents O₂ delivery → acidosis.
- **Nitrate → nitrite:** nitrite oxidises Hb → metHb (cannot carry O₂) → hypoxia.
- **Antu:** ↑ permeability of lung capillaries → massive pulmonary oedema + pleural effusion.
- **Fluoroacetate (1080) / Sodium fluoroacetamide (1081):** Lethal synthesis — converted to fluorocitrate → inhibits aconitase → blocks TCA cycle → citrate + lactate accumulate.
- **Organophosphates (OPs):** irreversible inhibition of cholinesterase → over-stimulation of cholinergic receptors → acute cholinergic syndrome.
- **Carbamates:** **reversible** cholinesterase inhibitors (so oximes are NOT given for carbamate poisoning).
- **DDT-type organochlorines:** alter sodium and potassium ion movement in nerve cells.
- **Cantharidin / Spanish fly:** strong irritant to both urinary AND gastrointestinal tract.
- **Phenol (carbolic acid):** weak corrosive (superficial ulcers); coagulative necrosis of tissue proteins → thickened gastric mucosa; CNS depression from above downward.

## 3. Antidote Categories & Examples

| Category | Example & Use |
|----------|---------------|
| **Adsorbent** | Charcoal (activated). |
| **Coater** | Egg albumin, paraffin oil (mercury poisoning). |
| **Entengler** | Cotton. |
| **Diluent** | Water (oxalate poisoning). |
| **Delayer** | Adrenaline (snake venom). |
| **True chemical antidote (for CTA)** | Citric acid / 5 % acetic acid / vinegar for alkalis; 2 % Na bicarbonate (externally), Mg oxide (internally), milk of magnesia for mineral acids. |
| **Precipitant antidote** | Ferric hydroxide → arsenic; egg albumin → mercury; magnesium sulphate → lead/phenol; tannic acid → many poisons. |
| **Oxidizing antidote** | Potassium permanganate or copper sulphate → inorganic yellow phosphorus poisoning. |
| **Reducing antidote** | Methylene blue → nitrate or chlorate poisoning. |
| **Specific antidote** | BAL, CaEDTA, oximes, atropine, etc. |
| **Chelating agents** | CaEDTA → lead (and nickel) ; D-penicillamine → copper ; BAL/DMSA → arsenic, mercury ; deferoxamine → iron. |

### Mechanism of action of antidotes
- **Chelation:** EDTA + Pb → PbEDTA (water-soluble) → excreted in urine; SH of BAL + arsenic.
- **Conversion to non-toxic product:** Hb + nitrite → metHb + cyanide → cyanmetHb; cyanmetHb + Na thiosulphate (rhodanase) → thiocyanate → urine.
- **↑ excretion:** sulphate ion → ↑ copper elimination in ruminants.
- **Receptor block:** atropine → muscarinic receptors → prevents O.P.C. action.
- **Receptor competition:** neostigmine vs curare; nalorphine vs morphine; Vit K vs coumarin.

### Supportive treatment
- Convulsion → pentobarbitone (iv → ip).
- Coma → amphetamine / methyl-amphetamine.
- Dehydration → normal saline / Ringer's lactate.
- Hypothermia → warm water.
- Hyperthermia → cold water or ice.
- Irritation → demulcents.

## 4. Heavy Metals

### Q (Compare): Acute clinical signs — Inorganic trivalent As vs Organic pentavalent As
- **Inorganic trivalent (acute):** salivation (prominent), thirst, vomiting, violent colic, watery (sometimes haemorrhagic) diarrhoea, may be ↓ °C, exhaustion, collapse, death within 1-3 days.
- **Organic pentavalent (early):** ataxia + incoordination in 2-4 days, appetite remains normal, blindness.

### Q (Compare): Acute Copper poisoning vs Chronic Copper of poisoned sheep
- **Acute Cu:** nausea, vomiting, salivation, purgation, violent abdominal pain, rapid dehydration, convulsion, paralysis → death. Faeces contain mucus and are deep green (copper-chlorophyll). Coagulation of blood at time of death is characteristic. TD 25-50 mg/kg in sheep.
- **Chronic Cu (sheep) — 3 stages:**
  1. 2-3 months — no clinical signs (storage in liver).
  2. 2-3 weeks — slight ↑ blood Cu, ↑ liver enzymes, anorexia, depression, diarrhoea.
  3. Acute haemolytic crisis (2-5 days) — acute anaemia, jaundice, haemoglobinaemia, haemoglobinuria, fever, dyspnoea. **Cause of most signs of toxicosis.**

### Q (Compare): Cold stiffness vs Heat stiffness
- **Cold stiffness:** °C ↓ → rigidity due to freezing/icing of synovial fluids; short rigor; gradual loss & secondary flaccidity → rigor mortis returns briefly.
- **Heat stiffness:** °C ↑ → coagulation of muscle proteins → rigor mortis comes on rapidly and passes off rapidly.

### Q (Compare): Mechanism of action — Copper vs Trivalent arsenical compounds
- **Copper:** excess Cu → storage in lysosomes & mitochondria → release → oxidises RBC membrane → ↑ fragility → haemolysis + hepatocellular necrosis; also oxidises Hb → metHb.
- **Trivalent arsenicals:** inhibition of cellular respiration (As + SH of dihydrolipoic acid → blocks acetyl/succinyl/propionyl-CoA formation) + capillary dilatation & degeneration.

### Q (Compare): Mechanism of action — Fluoride vs Molybdenum salts
- **Fluoride:** ↓ mineralisation of teeth & skeleton — damages ameloblasts/odontoblasts (developing teeth → defective enamel, brown/black discolouration); high Ca affinity → replaces hydroxyl groups in hydroxyapatite → exostosis / osteoporosis.
- **Mo salts:** promote hepatic Cu excretion by forming a readily-excreted Cu-Mo complex in urine → clinical Cu deficiency.

### Q (Compare): Lead poisoning — Horses vs Cattle
- **Cattle (acute, most common):** nervous signs 2-3 days → staggering, circling, frothing of the mouth, excitement, crash into objects, blindness → 2-3 hrs → death. Depression, ataxia, muscle twitching, rolling eyes, teeth grinding.
- **Cattle (chronic):** anorexia, blindness, constipation, recumbency → death.
- **Horses (chronic):** depression, loss of appetite/weight, muscular weakness, joint stiffness, colic, blindness, **"roaring"** (paralysis of recurrent laryngeal nerve), epiphyseal enlargement in colts ("lead-line").

### Q (Compare): Inorganic vs Organic mercury (dogs)
- **Inorganic (acute):** violent gastroenteritis, vomiting, diarrhoea, dehydration, shock / circulatory collapse → ventricular fibrillation → death in hours; if 1-2 days survived → stomatitis, acute nephritis, albuminuria.
- **Organic alkyl (dogs):** CNS excitability → blindness, convulsion, involuntary chewing/30 sec.

### Q (Compare): Selenium — Acute vs Chronic (Alkali disease)
- **Acute:** rapid & weak pulse, dyspnoea, bloating, colic, polyuria, cyanosis, prostration → death from respiratory failure.
- **Subacute "Blind staggers":** weight loss, poor coat, wandering, staggering gait + impaired vision → blindness, salivation, lacrimation, abdominal pain → paralysis → death.
- **Chronic (Alkali disease):** loss of long hair from mane & tail, rough coat, dullness, emaciation, abnormal horn/hoof growth, stiffness, lameness, infertility.

### Q (Compare): Cortical vs Brain-stem death
- **Cortical death:** if cortex alone is destroyed (drug effects, short hypoxia) — victim in deep coma but brain-stem still functions → sustains spontaneous respiration. Death eventually from extension of cerebral damage or intercurrent infection.
- **Brain-stem death:** brain-stem damage (trauma, cerebral oedema, haemorrhage, hypoxia, infection) → vital centres in medulla destroyed → respiratory motor system fails → both respiration & consciousness permanently lost. **= Legal death.**

### Q (Compare): Rigor mortis vs Cadaveric spasm
| Rigor mortis | Cadaveric spasm |
|---|---|
| Found in all dead bodies | Only in some (suicidal) cases |
| In all muscles | Some muscles (hand, masseter) |
| Starts after 1-2 h | Immediately at moment of death |
| Due to chemical changes (ATP↓ / lactic acid↑) | Due to nervous effect (high stress at moment of death) |

### Q (Compare): Degree of Burning vs Degree of Frostbite
| Burning | Frostbite |
|---|---|
| 1st: erythema + blistering (no scar) | 1st: redness & oedema (heals without permanent damage) |
| 2nd: full thickness of epidermis + dermal exposure (painful) | 2nd: epidermis only + blister formation |
| 3rd: destruction down to subdermal tissue + muscle/bone exposure, scar tissue | 3rd: skin + s/c tissue necrotised |
|  | 4th: total tissue loss including muscle & bone |

### Short notes — Treatment & antidotes (heavy metals)
- **Lead treatment:** Chelation — CaEDTA (110 mg/kg/day) 3 days, repeat 2 days later; Dimercaprol before CaEDTA crosses BBB; D-penicillamine for chronic. Supplemental — thiamine, corticosteroids, osmotic diuretics, Zn supplements, barbiturates. GI decontamination — Na/Mg sulphate cathartic; rumenotomy. Supportive.
- **Arsenic:** BAL 3 mg/kg IM (6× day 1-2 → 4× day 3 → 2× for 10 days). Thioctic acid 50 mg/kg IM. Fresh ferric hydroxide. Na thiosulphate orally.
- **Mercury:** Egg white or activated charcoal + saturated NaHCO₃ gastric lavage; oral Na thiosulphate; BAL or DMSA (water-soluble).
- **Copper (chronic):** Ammonium tetrathiomolybdate; Na sulphate. Prevention: Mo 2-4 ppm to sheep ration; ammonium molybdate + thiosulphate daily; 250 ppm Zn ↓ Cu accumulation.
- **Fluoride:** Aluminium sulphate/chloride, Ca aluminate, Ca carbonate (↓ absorption). Ineffective once skeletal/dental lesions developed.
- **Antimony:** Emetic/lavage; tannic acid, milk of lime, Mg oxide; BAL.
- **Phosphorus:** Apomorphine emesis; 1-2 % CuSO₄ gastric wash (forms inert Cu-phosphide); 0.1-0.2 % KMnO₄; purgative.
- **Zinc:** symptomatic + supportive; IV fluids, electrolytes, blood transfusion; CaEDTA (in dextrose) ↑ renal excretion; Cu supplementation (large animals).

## 5. Acids, Phenol & Gases

### Q (essay): Clinical picture of phenol toxicity
Transient pain & local anaesthesia; transient vomiting; **brownish carbolic patches in mouth**; thickening of gastric mucosa; **smell of phenol** on breath. Systemic: CNS depression from above downward.

### Q (essay): Special symptoms & signs of Nitric acid
Lips/tongue/teeth turn **yellow** (due to protein change); vomit yellow-brown; distention of abdomen (gas evolution); oliguria/anuria; albumin and casts may appear in urine.

### Q (essay): Treatment of oxalic acid poisoning
- Dicalcium phosphate, Ca hydroxide, Ca gluconate or Ca acetate orally → precipitate oxalic acid in stomach as Ca oxalate.
- Egg or milk as demulcents.
- Local — eyes: irrigate immediately for 15 min with copious water.

### Q (essay): Post-mortem picture of Sulphuric acid
- Charring & corrosion of mouth/oesophagus/stomach.
- Stomach wall black; perforation commonly in fundus; remaining wall inflamed & red.
- If survived a few days → evidence of repair/scar tissue.
- Liver & kidney → fatty change.

### Q (essay): Clinical signs of phosgene toxicity
- Acute respiratory & circulatory failure — common clinical findings → results in death from anoxia.
- Damage → **pulmonary hydrolysis of phosgene into HCl + CO₂**.

### Q (essay): Silo Fillers' disease in human
- Occurs in workers entering unopened grain silos within a few hours of decomposition. Higher nitrate content → higher NO₂ concentration → released into air (also CO₂).

### Q (essay): Mechanism of action of CO₂ toxicity
- CO₂ is normally produced in body and released from lung.
- High alveolar CO₂ prevents further CO₂ release → CO₂ retention → acidosis.
- ↑ alveolar ventilation → changed pH.

### Q (essay): Clinical signs of Acrolein
- Acute exposure to acrolein vapors → lacrimation + severe respiratory irritation. Reacts with **sulfhydryl group** in blood/tissue. Disrupts intermediary metabolism, DNA replication, protein synthesis.

### Q (essay): Treatment of HCN poisoning
1. Amyl nitrite (inhaled).
2. **Nitrites + OxyHb → MetHb** (cyanide trap).
3. **MetHb + cyanide → CyanoMetHb** (cytochrome oxidase freed).
4. **CyanoMetHb + Na thiosulphate → Na thiocyanate** (excreted in urine) + MetHb.
5. **MetHb + ascorbic acid → OxyHb** (regenerate Hb).
6. Hydroxocobalamine; gastric lavage.

### Q (essay): Treatment & prevention of Aflatoxin
- **Detoxification:** Hydrated Sodium Calcium Aluminosilicate (HSCAS).
- **Supportive:** Vit E + Selenium.
- **Prevention:** mould inhibitors; treat grain with anhydrous ammonia for 10-14 days to reduce aflatoxin.

### Q (essay): Treatment of Ammonia (NH₃) in birds
- Remove affected birds to clean, well-ventilated houses.
- Maintain adequate ventilation. (Eyes irrigate; supportive.)

### Q (essay): Clinical signs of Spanish fly (Cantharidin)
- Acute severe colic ± shock; death may occur within 2-3 days.
- GI ulceration → diarrhoea, bloody faeces, or both.
- Urinary irritation.

## 6. Forensic Medicine

### Q (Short notes from exams)

**Bruises.** Caused by blunt injury damaging blood vessels (small veins/arterioles) beneath the surface; blood leaks into surrounding tissues. Colour change blue → brown → green → yellow → normal. Cannot be inflicted on a dead body.

**Abrasions.** Forensically most important — retain the pattern of the causative object. Most superficial type of injury (damages only the cuticle/epidermis). Caused by tangential glancing impact. May be linear (simple scratch) or broad (graze/brush). Common cause: falling with sliding motion ("gravel rash"). Numerous parallel linear abrasions = "friction burn".

**Cold edema.** Cold diuresis + leaking of plasma into extracellular spaces — haemoconcentration.

**Primary flaccidity & contact flattening.** Immediately after death → loss of muscular tone, loss of muscular elasticity, flattening of convex parts (contact flattening), loss of eye tension → protrusion and dilatation of eye pupil.

**Mechanism of ethylene glycol.** High levels of Na in brain inhibit anaerobic glycolysis; energy is required to actively transport Na out of CNS. Oxidised to oxalic acid → metabolic acidosis & renal injury (Ca oxalate crystals).

**Water deprivation (Na ion) toxicosis.** Na enters brain by passive diffusion; removed by active transport. Limited H₂O intake or dehydration → blood plasma Na ↑ 150-190; CSF Na 145-185 → neurological signs.

**Suffocation.** Absence/reduction of O₂ in inspired air OR mechanical obstruction to entry of air into external respiratory orifices.

**Lacerations.** Most dangerous type of injury (also called "gashes" or "cuts"). Splitting/tearing wound from blunt impact passing through full thickness of skin → bleeds profusely. Tissues crushed in sandwich fashion.

**Clinical signs of water intoxication.** Intermittent convulsive seizures → continuous convulsions, circling, severe CNS depression, blindness, head pressing → terminal: lateral recumbency and paddling motion of all limbs.

**Mechanism of action of Antu.** Causes increased permeability of lung capillaries → massive pulmonary oedema and pleural effusion.

**Mechanism of action of CO.** CO competes with O₂ for binding to Hb (affinity 240× greater); COHb prevents release of O₂ at tissue & impairs CO₂ removal → acidosis → cellular hypoxia/anoxia.

**Clinical signs of inorganic Hg poisoned cattle.** ↓ °C, colic → rapid death.

**Clinical signs of chronic Selenium poisoned cattle.** Loss of long hair from mane & tail; rough coat; dullness; lack of vitality; emaciation; abnormal horn/hoof growth/structure; stiffness; lameness; infertility.

**Delayed effect of radiation on animals.** After few months → ↑ incidence of leukemia, shortening of life span, teratogenic effect, little effect on fertility.

**Complication of frostbite.** (1) Fat embolism can be an early complication of severe frostbite → may lead to immediate death. (2) Later: skin discoloration, cold sensitivity, pain, atrophy, keratinisation of skin.

**Heat cramps / Miner's cramp / Stoker's cramp.** Painful spasms in voluntary muscles (esp. extremities & abdominal wall) during/after physical activity in hot environment → rapid dehydration + loss of salts. IV physiological saline → dramatic relief; salt tablets + water = prophylactic.

**Sources of nitrate/nitrite poisoning.** Artificial manures & urine (Na nitrate, ammonium nitrate, K nitrate); well water (contaminated by decaying organic matter, fertiliser, animal wastes, silage juice, nitrogen-fixing bacteria); plant nitrates (weeds & crops grown under poor conditions); silage (silo gases).

**Causes of brain-stem death.** Trauma, cerebral oedema, haemorrhage, hypoxia, infection → vital centres in medulla destroyed → respiratory motor system fails (= legal death).

**Clinical signs of Sulphuric acid poisoning.** Tongue swollen with white coating; teeth chalky white, lose polish; lips swollen & excoriated; salivation; urine may be blue; vomit brown to black; laryngo-/broncho-spasm → main cause of death.

**Clinical signs of Dinitro compounds.** Same in all animals — within few minutes to hours: loss of appetite, ↑ activity, deep & rapid respiration, sweating, thirst, oliguria, muscular weakness, dyspnoea, terminal hyperthermia. Skin/hair may be yellow-stained; urine yellowish-green.

**Diagnosis of Fluoroacetate poisoning.** Case history; clinical signs; chemical analysis of bait/vomitus; gas-liquid chromatography. **Tissue citric acid level 3× normal is diagnostic**; serum glucose elevated.

**Hide-and-die syndrome.** ↓ °C in old people + psychiatric component → victim hides in cupboards/corners/under furniture, removes some or all clothing → hypothermic.

**Wallace's rule (Rule of nine).** Head & neck 9 % | each fore limb 9 % | trunk each side 18 % | hind limbs 18 % | external genitalia 1 %. Area > 50 % burned even 1st degree → poor prognosis.

**Sources of oxalic acid/oxalate poisoning in domestic animals.** Plants — *Rumex* spp, *Beta vulgaris* (sugar beet), *Oxalis* spp, *Avocado*; antifreeze (ethylene glycol metabolised to oxalic acid).

**Post-mortem rigidity (Rigor mortis).** Irreversible combination of actin & myosin within skeletal/cardiac/involuntary muscle (ATP↓, lactic acid↑). Starts in eye lids → masseter → trunk → fore limbs → abdomen → hind limbs; 1-4 h face, 4-6 h limbs; disappears 18-50 h. Cold delays but prolongs; exhausted muscle → quick RM; strong muscle → delayed RM.

**Roentgen.** Unit of radiation exposure — amount of radiation that produces one esu of charge per cm³ of air at STP (X- or γ-rays only). 1 R = 2.58 × 10⁻⁴ C·kg⁻¹.

**Heat haematoma.** See definitions.

**Mechanism of action of cyanide.** See definitions.

**Treatment of acute cholinergic syndrome of OPs.**
1. Decontamination (skin wash, GI decontamination).
2. Emesis or gastric lavage.
3. Activated charcoal.
4. **Atropine sulphate** — anti-muscarinic; block muscarinic effects.
5. **Oxime (2-PAM)** — reactivates phosphorylated cholinesterase (if not aged).

**Mechanism of action of Sodium acetamide (1081).** Same as fluoroacetate — metabolised to fluorocitrate → inhibits aconitase → blocks TCA cycle → citrate & lactate accumulate.

**Clinical signs of chronic Cu-poisoned sheep.** See "Heavy Metals — Cu chronic".

**Factors affecting nitrate levels in plants.** Species of plant (weeds & crops); content & form of N in soil; soil conditions; light (activates nitrate reductase); drought; herbicide treatment (2,4-D speeds growth and nitrate accumulation).

**Treatment of nitrate-poisoned animals.** Methylene blue 1-2 % aqueous solution 1-2 mg/kg IV (treated animals must not be slaughtered for 180 days; tissues & urine stained dark green). Mineral oil (via stomach tube). Purgatives. Antibiotics to control bacterial nitrate reduction.

**Early effects of whole-body radiation.** Several thousand rad → CNS effect → death within hours. 500-2000 rad → GI injury within several hours. 100-500 rad → GI signs + depression in days.

**Choking.** Internal obstruction of upper air passages by an impacted object/substance → severe respiratory distress, congestion, stenosis, petechiae → rapid silent death from vaso-vagal cardiac arrest (also called *café coronary* when food bolus).

**Abraded wound.** See "Abrasion".

**Adipocere formation.** Wet conditions; body fat → hydrolysis → waxy compound similar to soap. Weeks to months. First stage: greasy pale rancid butter (smell); after years grey-white brittle/crumbling like cheese. Often patchy/partial. Helps preserve s/c & abdominal fat for decades.

**Factors affecting putrefaction.**
- Temperature: in air twice as fast as in water.
- Air (O₂): in air ≥ 4× faster than buried in the ground.
- Humidity: haemorrhage/dehydration → ↓ putrefaction.
- Age: newborn ↓ putrefaction (lack of bacteria).
- Cause of death: septicaemia ↑ putrefaction.
- Predators: outside body → flies/maggots → hasten putrefaction.

**Factors affecting heat loss of dead animals.**
1. Difference between body & environmental temperature.
2. Highest rate of loss immediately after death.
3. Bodies in water cool faster than in air.
4. Young cool faster than old.
5. Fattened animals cool slower than non-fattened.
6. Some bodies maintain temperature: infectious disease, sunstroke, cerebral injury, strychnine poisoning, tetanus.

**Cortical Brain Death vs Brain-Stem Death.** See above (Death definitions).

**Difference Throttling vs Ligature strangulation.** **Throttling** = manual; abrasions/disc-shaped bruises on front & sides of neck; classic mode of male-on-female homicide; rapid or prolonged death (with classic signs and petechiae from nose/ear). **Ligature** = constricting band; leaves a patterned vital mark (sharp & clear with wire/thin cord; diffuse with soft fabric); congestion/cyanosis/petechiae of face; less internal damage than manual. **Hanging** is a ligature subtype where pressure is by body weight.

**Washerwoman's fingers.** See definitions — non-specific immersion sign.

**Diatom test.** See definitions — suggestive only.

**Dry vs Wet starvation.** Dry = no food + no water → no oedema → hypotension → cardiac failure (~10 days). Wet = no food but water available → oedema/ascites, hypoproteinaemia (~50-60 days).

**Blood boils out of venous sinuses → external hematoma.** = Heat haematoma (see definitions).

**Wound vs Injury (law).** In medical sense they mean the same; in law a "wound" suggests a deliberate action; an "injury" can arise from any cause including pure accident.

**Contact wound.** Muzzle touches skin → circular abrasion, smoke soiling inside, tissue blackened, pink rim from CO, wad/bullet inside. Over bone: gases force skin to split → entry wound ragged/irregular.

**Exit wound (rifled weapon).** Everted with split flaps → stellate appearance. No smoke/burning/powder. Distorted/struck-bone bullet → larger irregular exit. With high-velocity missile striking bone → tear large defect.

**Low velocity (revolver, sport gun) vs High velocity (military) missiles.** Low — mechanical disruption by pushing tissue aside + damage from expanding gas in close range; High — transfers energy laterally along track, especially destructive in dense organs (liver, brain).
